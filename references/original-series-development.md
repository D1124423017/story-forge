# Original Series Development

Use this reference when the user wants a commercially strong original novel, high-concept premise, series bible, chapter engine, cast architecture, or original transformation of inspiration from famous works. The goal is not to promise success; the goal is to build the kind of story engine that can plausibly sustain wide reader interest.

## Core Standard

A strong original series needs:

* high-concept clarity: a one-sentence premise that creates immediate curiosity
* universal emotional engine: loneliness, belonging, grief, shame, ambition, first love, family fracture, forbidden desire, fear of death, hunger for freedom
* expandable world engine: rules, costs, institutions, rituals, taboos, professions, objects, locations, holidays, competitions, dangers
* cast engine: protagonist, friend group, rival, mentor, antagonist, comic pressure, authority figure, betrayer, and wildcard
* mystery engine: questions that can resolve locally while opening larger questions
* chapter engine: every chapter changes danger, knowledge, emotion, trust, or desire
* series engine: each book has its own promise while escalating the larger conflict
* originality engine: the story transforms inspirations into new logic, not recognizable imitation

## High-Concept Premise Builder

Build the premise in this order:

1. Ordinary wound: What human pain makes the reader care before the worldbuilding begins?
2. Extraordinary doorway: What impossible system, place, power, social order, or danger enters the character's life?
3. Desire: What does the protagonist want badly enough to act?
4. Cost: What does the world demand in exchange?
5. Opposition: Who or what profits from keeping the truth hidden?
6. Series question: What larger mystery or conflict can sustain multiple books?

Useful format:

```text
When [wounded protagonist] discovers [extraordinary system], they must [urgent goal], but every step costs [personal price] and exposes [larger secret].
```

Reject premises that are only setting descriptions. A moving city, secret academy, cursed kingdom, alien archive, or immortal court is not enough. Ask who is hurt by it, who needs it, who lies about it, and what it costs to belong.

## Commercial Hook Audit

Check whether the story has:

* clear audience promise: what pleasure does the reader expect?
* emotional accessibility: can the reader understand the wound without a lore lecture?
* recurring pleasures: classes, trials, competitions, rituals, investigations, missions, festivals, forbidden rooms, faction politics, special objects
* escalation ladder: each installment raises intimacy, danger, knowledge, moral cost, and scale
* iconic specificity: names, objects, institutions, and rituals that feel vivid but not derivative
* readability: scenes dramatize pressure instead of explaining systems
* franchise space: enough locations, roles, mysteries, and conflicts to generate more stories

If the idea is cool but emotionally thin, say so directly. Then attach the setting to a wound, a relationship, a moral test, and a personal cost.

## Series-Capable World Rules

Worldbuilding should create pressure, not decoration.

For each major world rule, define:

* rule: what is true in this world?
* limit: what cannot be done?
* cost: what does use, entry, knowledge, or status demand?
* institution: who controls or teaches it?
* black market: who abuses or sells it?
* taboo: what is forbidden and why?
* ritual: what repeated action makes the world memorable?
* inequality: who benefits and who is excluded?
* plot use: how does this rule create scenes, choices, and consequences?
* emotional use: how does this rule pressure the protagonist's wound?

Avoid free power. A world rule without cost becomes convenience. A world rule without social consequence becomes trivia.

## Long-Term Cast Architecture

Design the cast as a pressure system.

### Protagonist

Define:

* external goal
* private wound
* false belief
* talent
* flaw
* temptation
* relationship need
* moral line
* book-one change
* series-long change

### Friend Group

Give each friend:

* a separate competence
* a separate wound
* a conflict with the protagonist
* a reason to stay
* a reason to leave
* a secret or pressure that can drive a later book

Friend groups should not exist only to admire the protagonist.

### Rival

The rival should expose what the protagonist lacks, envies, fears, or could become. A strong rival is not merely mean; they are a competing answer to the same wound.

### Mentor

The mentor should offer access and distortion. They know something useful, hide something costly, and eventually force the protagonist to outgrow them.

### Antagonist Ladder

Build antagonists in layers:

* local antagonist: creates immediate chapter pressure
* institutional antagonist: reveals the system is unfair or corrupt
* intimate antagonist: wounds the protagonist personally
* mythic or series antagonist: embodies the central fear or false promise of the world

Each antagonist should want something specific. Do not make evil a personality substitute.

## Chapter Engine

Every chapter should earn its place.

Use this beat pattern when building a commercial chapter:

1. opening pressure: unresolved problem, desire, question, threat, or social risk
2. scene promise: what the reader expects to learn, fear, or see tested
3. active pursuit: the protagonist tries something
4. friction: a person, rule, secret, injury, deadline, or social cost blocks progress
5. discovery or reversal: new information changes the situation
6. choice: the character acts under pressure
7. cost: the choice damages safety, trust, status, body, belief, or future options
8. residue: the emotional consequence lingers
9. hook: a new question, danger, decision, betrayal, or image pulls the reader forward

If a chapter only explains lore, convert the lore into a trial, argument, punishment, game, investigation, ceremony, or failure.

## Book and Series Architecture

Book one should deliver:

* a complete local plot
* a visible emotional arc
* a memorable entry into the world
* at least one central mystery resolved
* at least one larger mystery opened
* a cast bond the reader wants to revisit
* a final cost that proves the story is not consequence-free

A series should escalate:

* Book 1: entry, belonging, first mystery, first cost
* Book 2: rules deepen, trust fractures, rival or institution becomes more dangerous
* Book 3: private wound becomes public consequence
* Book 4: the safe structure collapses or is revealed as compromised
* Book 5+: war, sacrifice, moral ambiguity, legacy, death, irreversible change

Adjust the number of installments to the user's project. Do not force a fixed length.

## Anti-Imitation Transformation

When the user references a famous work, separate structure from expression.

Do not copy:

* names, terminology, spells, houses, factions, institutions, species, artifacts, locations, scene sequences, iconic character roles, signature prose style, or recognizable plot turns

Allowed abstraction:

* hidden world
* school or training structure
* found family
* annual mystery
* ritualized competition
* coming-of-age escalation
* mentor secrecy
* corrupt institution
* chosen burden
* symbolic object
* villain tied to protagonist wound

Transformation process:

1. Identify the abstract narrative function.
2. Replace the premise logic.
3. Replace the social system.
4. Replace the power rules and costs.
5. Replace the cast relationships.
6. Replace the central wound.
7. Replace the mystery engine.
8. Replace the imagery, rituals, and vocabulary.
9. Test whether a reader would describe the result as its own thing.

If the answer still sounds like a reskin, reject it and rebuild from the wound and world cost.
