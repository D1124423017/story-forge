# Revision Rubric

Use this rubric when the user asks for scoring, strict critique, prioritization, or a diagnosis of what to revise first. Score each item from 1 to 5, then total out of 50.

## Tension

## Score 1

No meaningful pressure. Characters move through events without danger, desire, resistance, uncertainty, or consequence.

## Score 2

There is a basic threat or problem, but it stays static, distant, or too easy to avoid.

## Score 3

The scene has usable tension, but pressure does not escalate enough or lacks personal stakes.

## Score 4

Strong pressure with clear stakes, obstacles, and escalation; only a few beats could be sharpened.

## Score 5

Professional-level tension. Every beat tightens danger, desire, power, knowledge, or emotional risk.

## Clarity

## Score 1

The reader cannot track who wants what, where characters are, what happened, or why it matters.

## Score 2

Basic meaning is visible, but spatial logic, cause and effect, or character intention is often muddy.

## Score 3

Readable and mostly clear, but some transitions, motivations, or action beats need stronger orientation.

## Score 4

Clear, controlled, and easy to follow; only minor tightening needed.

## Score 5

Crystal clear without over-explaining. The reader always knows the dramatic situation and stakes.

## Character Agency

## Score 1

Characters are passive objects moved by plot convenience.

## Score 2

Characters show intent but rarely make meaningful choices that change outcomes.

## Score 3

Characters act and choose, but their decisions could carry stronger risk, cost, or specificity.

## Score 4

Characters make active, motivated choices that shape plot and relationships.

## Score 5

Agency is excellent. Choices reveal desire, fear, contradiction, moral pressure, and consequence.

## Dialogue

## Score 1

Dialogue is stiff, interchangeable, expository, or speech-like.

## Score 2

Some lines work, but characters often explain the plot or sound too similar.

## Score 3

Dialogue is functional and readable, with occasional subtext or voice, but lacks consistent edge.

## Score 4

Natural, character-specific dialogue with pressure, rhythm, and subtext.

## Score 5

Dialogue feels alive. Every line reveals character, relationship, power, evasion, or emotional truth.

## Prose Texture

## Score 1

Prose is abstract, generic, flat, or overloaded with summary.

## Score 2

Some concrete detail exists, but the writing relies heavily on labels, explanation, or cliché.

## Score 3

Competent prose with moments of image or rhythm, but not yet distinctive or immersive.

## Score 4

Strong sensory detail, sentence control, and voice with minor rough spots.

## Score 5

Professional prose: vivid, precise, emotionally charged, and controlled without showing off.

## Pacing

## Score 1

The section drags, rushes, or reads like a list of events with no dramatic shaping.

## Score 2

The pace has intent but weak transitions, overlong setup, or compressed emotional beats.

## Score 3

Usable rhythm, though some moments should be cut, expanded, delayed, or accelerated.

## Score 4

Strong pacing with effective contrast, escalation, and scene turns.

## Score 5

Expert pacing. The draft controls attention, breath, suspense, release, and hooks with precision.

## Emotional Impact

## Score 1

The reader is told what characters feel but does not feel it.

## Score 2

Emotion is present but generic, melodramatic, or disconnected from action.

## Score 3

The emotional intent is clear and sometimes lands, but needs more subtext, specificity, or cost.

## Score 4

Emotion is grounded, specific, and tied to character choices.

## Score 5

Deep emotional impact. The scene leaves residue through image, behavior, silence, and consequence.

## Originality

## Score 1

The material feels copied from familiar tropes without transformation.

## Score 2

There is a fresh element, but cliché still drives most turns, characters, or images.

## Score 3

Some fresh choices appear, though the draft still leans on expected beats.

## Score 4

The piece uses genre expectations well while adding distinct perspective, situation, or voice.

## Score 5

Fresh and inevitable. The story honors genre promise while producing memorable surprises.

## Continuity

## Score 1

Names, timeline, rules, injuries, locations, or relationships contradict the established story.

## Score 2

Continuity mostly exists, but repeated small slips weaken trust.

## Score 3

Generally consistent, with a few issues that need cleanup.

## Score 4

Strong continuity; prior events and rules are respected with minor gaps.

## Score 5

Excellent continuity. The draft actively uses prior details, promises, costs, and consequences.

## Commercial Readability

## Score 1

The draft is difficult to enter, unclear in appeal, or too unfocused for most target readers.

## Score 2

There is a marketable idea, but execution blurs hook, stakes, genre promise, or readability.

## Score 3

Readable and viable, but needs stronger hooks, cleaner structure, or more distinctive scenes.

## Score 4

Strong reader appeal with clear promise, momentum, and accessible prose.

## Score 5

Highly readable and commercially sharp. The draft has strong hook, momentum, genre delivery, and emotional payoff.
