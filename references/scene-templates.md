# Scene Templates

Use these templates as flexible beat maps, not rigid formulas. Adapt order, intensity, and emphasis to the user's genre, point of view, tone, and canon.

## Action Scene

* spatial setup: Establish exits, obstacles, distances, weapons, cover, light, noise, and who can see whom.
* immediate threat: Put danger in motion before explaining everything.
* character objective: Define what the character must do besides "win": escape, protect, delay, retrieve, silence, expose.
* obstacle: Add terrain, injury, fear, a hostage, limited ammo, moral hesitation, or a stronger opponent.
* physical exchange: Use clear cause and effect: strike, dodge, impact, consequence, adjustment.
* injury or cost: Make the body remember the scene through pain, fatigue, lost equipment, exposure, or guilt.
* turn: Change the rules, reveal a hidden threat, break the plan, or force a new tactic.
* aftermath: Show breath, blood, damage, silence, shame, relief, missing people, or the next danger.

## Horror Scene

* false safety: Begin with a routine, shelter, familiar person, plausible explanation, or moment of relief.
* sensory disturbance: Add a wrong sound, smell, texture, temperature, reflection, shadow, rhythm, or bodily sensation.
* delayed reveal: Let characters interpret traces before seeing the source.
* wrong explanation: Offer a rational explanation that almost works.
* irreversible sign: Show proof that cannot be dismissed: a changed body, impossible message, missing time, altered room, infected wound.
* confrontation: Force the character to face the threat, but keep some uncertainty alive.
* residue: End with contamination, fear, silence, guilt, obsession, or a new rule.

## Argument Scene

* surface topic: Start with what they claim to be arguing about.
* real emotional issue: Identify the hidden wound: abandonment, disrespect, jealousy, fear, shame, betrayal.
* power imbalance: Decide who has authority, information, moral leverage, money, status, or emotional control.
* escalation: Let each line become more specific, personal, or dangerous.
* line crossed: Someone says the thing that cannot be unsaid.
* silence or interruption: Use a pause, entrance, sound, violence, laughter, or practical crisis to change rhythm.
* changed relationship: End with less trust, new honesty, a broken alliance, or a reluctant compromise.

## Confession Scene

* reason for delay: The character has avoided this truth because of fear, shame, duty, timing, or danger.
* vulnerability: The confession should expose need, guilt, love, weakness, or responsibility.
* interruption risk: Add a person, deadline, noise, danger, or emotional collapse that could stop the confession.
* incomplete honesty: Let the character reveal enough to matter but possibly still hold one thing back.
* response: The listener may accept, reject, deflect, misunderstand, weaponize, or quietly absorb it.
* consequence: The confession should alter trust, plans, intimacy, danger, or self-image.

## Betrayal Scene

* trust setup: Remind the reader what made trust possible: shared history, intimacy, oath, rescue, dependence, routine.
* hidden motive: Give the betrayer a motive that feels human, not merely evil.
* reveal: Use action, evidence, overheard speech, missing object, timing, or a direct admission.
* denial or justification: Let the betrayer lie, minimize, rationalize, accuse, or confess selectively.
* emotional wound: Show the betrayed person's specific hurt: humiliation, grief, rage, self-blame, numbness.
* practical consequence: The betrayal should change safety, resources, alliances, public reputation, or mission success.
* new alignment: End with a changed map of enemies, allies, debts, and vulnerabilities.

## Quiet Aftermath Scene

* physical cleanup: Bandage, wash, bury, repair, count supplies, wipe blood, collect broken things.
* silence: Let the absence of speech carry what characters cannot face.
* small action: Use a cigarette, cup, blanket, shoelace, weapon check, folded note, or shared food.
* avoided topic: Characters talk around the true emotional center.
* emotional leak: A gesture, mistake, joke, flinch, repeated phrase, or sudden anger reveals feeling.
* changed bond: Someone offers help, refuses touch, uses a new name, sits closer, leaves first, or stops pretending.
* next pressure: End with the next problem entering quietly or becoming impossible to ignore.

## Chapter Ending Hook

* emotional hook: End on a feeling the reader wants resolved: guilt, longing, dread, shame, tenderness, rage.
* danger hook: End with an immediate threat, a countdown, a sound at the door, a failed defense, a body missing.
* information hook: Reveal a clue, contradiction, message, name, map, recording, or impossible fact.
* betrayal hook: Show that someone lied, switched sides, hid evidence, or chose another loyalty.
* decision hook: End at the moment a character chooses, or just after a choice creates cost.
* image hook: Leave a vivid object or image that implies story: a wet footprint inside a locked room, a wedding ring in ash, a child's drawing of tomorrow's murder.

## Moral Choice Scene

* two bad options: Make both choices costly and defensible.
* personal stake: Tie the choice to someone the character loves, hates, owes, fears, or wants to become.
* public stake: Show who else suffers or benefits.
* time pressure: Limit debate with danger, deadline, injury, vote, weather, pursuit, or public exposure.
* argument: Let different values clash: mercy, survival, law, loyalty, truth, revenge, freedom.
* decision: The character must act, not have the choice removed by coincidence.
* cost: The decision should damage something real.
* emotional residue: End with guilt, justification, silence, changed trust, or a self-image fracture.

## Group Conflict Scene

* resource pressure: Food, water, medicine, space, ammo, money, attention, status, or information is insufficient.
* competing values: Survival vs mercy, truth vs morale, speed vs safety, loyalty vs justice, tradition vs change.
* spokespersons: Give each side a person with a clear motive, wound, and rhetorical style.
* rumor or fear: Let incomplete information spread faster than truth.
* escalation: Move from complaint to accusation to threat to action.
* authority test: Someone challenges leadership, procedure, expertise, age, class, or moral standing.
* compromise or fracture: End with a temporary solution, split, exile, coup, secret plan, or public humiliation.

## Boss Fight Scene

* boss presence: Establish the boss through silhouette, scale, ritual, reputation, environment, voice, or prior damage.
* rule discovery: The heroes learn what does not work and what pattern governs the fight.
* failed first strategy: Let the first plan break quickly and painfully.
* injury or sacrifice: Someone pays for knowledge, time, or an opening.
* adaptation: The heroes use environment, memory, teamwork, weakness, timing, or emotional insight.
* risky plan: The winning tactic should expose the character to danger or moral cost.
* killing blow: Make the decisive moment clear, earned, and tied to prior setup.
* aftermath: Show silence, damage, relief, disbelief, grief, or the boss's final effect on the world.
* cost: Track who is hurt, what is lost, what promise broke, or what resource is gone.
* lingering mystery: Leave one mark, phrase, object, mutation, or clue that implies the fight did not answer everything.
