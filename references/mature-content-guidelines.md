# Mature Content Guidelines

Use this reference when the user wants 18+ fiction, adult psychological tension, dark romance, intimacy, trauma aftermath, horror, violence, addiction, grief, coercive institutions, moral ugliness, or other mature themes. The goal is to make mature material emotionally truthful and narratively necessary, not merely more explicit.

## Core Rule

Mature content must serve character, conflict, atmosphere, consequence, or theme.

Do not treat 18+ material as decoration. Ask what it reveals, costs, damages, tempts, heals, corrupts, or changes.

## Adult-Only Intimacy Standard

For sexual or intimate material:

* Keep all participants clearly adult.
* Preserve consent, agency, and the ability to refuse.
* Track power imbalance: status, money, age gap between adults, captivity, debt, authority, fame, employment, magic, intoxication, injury, dependence, or blackmail.
* If power imbalance exists, make it part of the conflict and consequence; do not pretend it is automatically romantic.
* Do not eroticize minors, assault, coercion, exploitation, trafficking, intoxicated inability to consent, or unconsciousness.
* When the user's desired explicitness is unclear, use restrained literary treatment or ask for heat level.

## Heat Levels

Use heat levels to control intensity:

* Level 1: charged subtext, glances, restraint, proximity, almost-confession
* Level 2: explicit desire and emotional vulnerability without graphic description
* Level 3: sensual scene focused on psychology, consent, and consequence; avoid anatomical detail unless the user explicitly asks and the content remains adult and consensual
* Level 4: fade-to-black after clear emotional and consent beats

Default to Level 1 or 2 unless the user asks for more.

## Dark Romance and Power Imbalance

Dark romance can include danger, obsession, guilt, manipulation, revenge, class pressure, secrecy, betrayal, or morally compromised attraction. The craft problem is not darkness; the problem is pretending harm has no cost.

When writing dark romance:

* Give both adults interiority and agency.
* Make desire conflict with fear, pride, guilt, loyalty, survival, or self-respect.
* Let consent be visible in behavior, not only implied by genre convention.
* Show consequences: trust damage, shame, leverage, changed power, social risk, emotional residue.
* Avoid making coercion look like proof of love.
* Let the endangered character resist, negotiate, misread, choose, retreat, retaliate, or set terms.

## Adult Psychological Tension

Build mature tension through:

* what the character wants but refuses to admit
* what they can take but should not
* what consent, debt, status, guilt, or loyalty complicates
* what each character knows about the other's wound
* what line would change the relationship permanently
* what silence, distance, touch, refusal, or almost-touch means
* what the scene costs afterward

Useful scene rhythm:

pressure -> attraction or threat -> boundary -> evasion -> truth leak -> choice -> cost -> residue

## Trauma and Aftermath

When handling trauma:

* Focus on aftermath, behavior, memory, avoidance, numbness, anger, shame, control, distrust, and recovery pressure.
* Do not use trauma only as backstory seasoning.
* Do not make another character's attraction "cure" trauma.
* Give the character choices and survival strategies, even if imperfect.
* Let recovery be uneven and socially complicated.

## Violence and Horror

For mature violence:

* Make the body, space, fear, confusion, and aftermath matter.
* Use gore only when it changes emotion, danger, or theme.
* Track injury, exhaustion, shock, guilt, revenge, and moral consequence.
* Do not let brutality become repetitive decoration.

For horror:

* Delay certainty.
* Distort senses.
* Let the threat violate safety, body, memory, identity, or trust.
* Keep irreversible consequences.

## Mature Theme Checklist

Before finalizing mature material, check:

* Are all intimate participants adults?
* Is consent or lack of consent handled honestly and not glamorized?
* Does the scene create character change or consequence?
* Does the power imbalance matter on the page?
* Is the darkness tied to theme, not just shock?
* Is there emotional residue after the scene?
* Does the scene preserve agency?
* Is explicitness calibrated to the user's request and the story's needs?
