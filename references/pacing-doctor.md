# Pacing Doctor

Use this reference when the user asks why a story feels slow, rushed, flat, confusing, too explanatory, too breathless, or not page-turning. Diagnose pacing by level before rewriting: scene, chapter, whole book, emotional, information, and genre rhythm.

## Core Principle

Pacing is not speed. Pacing is control over pressure, attention, breath, expectation, release, and emotional weight.

A strong story knows when to accelerate and when to slow down:

* accelerate when danger, pursuit, decision, argument, combat, or irreversible action is active
* slow down when emotion, betrayal, death, confession, trauma, intimacy, failure, revelation, or moral cost needs to land
* alternate high pressure with contrast so the reader can feel escalation instead of numbness
* reveal information through action, clues, errors, cost, and consequences instead of blocks of explanation

## Pacing Diagnosis Workflow

When diagnosing pacing, identify:

1. level: scene, chapter, whole book, emotional beat, information reveal, or genre rhythm
2. symptom: dragging, rushing, event-listing, flat middle, early exposition, skipped residue, weak hook, repeated beat, or climax too easy
3. cause: missing pressure, weak objective, no obstacle, no turn, no cost, too much summary, misplaced reveal, insufficient aftermath, monotone intensity
4. fix: cut, compress, expand, reorder, delay reveal, reveal earlier, add contrast, sharpen hook, add cost, add residue, or restructure chapter blocks

Use this format when useful:

```text
【節奏問題】

【層級】

【原因】

【改法】

【重排版本 / 可直接替換版本】
```

## Scene Pacing

A scene should not be a list of things that happened.

Strong scene rhythm:

```text
entry pressure -> character objective -> obstacle -> friction -> turn -> choice -> cost -> emotional residue -> next pressure
```

Check:

* entry pressure: Does the scene start with a problem, desire, risk, deadline, awkwardness, danger, or unanswered question?
* objective: What does the viewpoint character want in this scene?
* obstacle: What blocks them?
* turn: What changes the situation?
* choice: What does the character do under pressure?
* cost: What gets worse because of the choice?
* residue: What feeling, image, wound, guilt, fear, desire, or changed trust remains?

Common fixes:

* Cut throat-clearing: waking up, eating, walking, dressing, travel, and logistics unless they carry pressure.
* Enter later: start closer to the moment of risk.
* Move threat earlier: do not wait until the final paragraph to reveal why the scene matters.
* Expand the turn: slow down when the character discovers, loses, betrays, chooses, or understands something.
* End earlier: leave on the turn, cost, image, or next pressure.

## Chapter Pacing

A chapter should be a unit of reader hunger.

Chapter rhythm:

```text
opening hook -> complication -> escalation or reveal -> choice -> cost -> ending hook
```

Check:

* opening hook: Does page one create a question, danger, desire, social risk, emotional pressure, or strange image?
* middle movement: Does the chapter escalate, reveal, reverse, or deepen conflict?
* ending hook: Does the chapter end on emotion, danger, information, betrayal, decision, or a strong image?
* forward motion: Is the next chapter harder because this chapter happened?
* residue: Does the reader carry a feeling forward?

Common fixes:

* Replace front-loaded worldbuilding with a problem inside the world.
* Split overloaded chapters into pressure units.
* Move a reveal to the midpoint if the chapter sags.
* Delay a reveal if the mystery dies too soon.
* Add an ending hook if the chapter simply stops.

## Whole-Book Pacing

A full book cannot be one emotional speed.

Avoid:

* nonstop crisis that numbs the reader
* endless setup before the story starts
* six consecutive lore chapters
* repeated fight scenes with no relationship or information change
* romance beats that jump from attraction to confession without pressure
* climax that resolves faster than the buildup deserves

Use alternating movement:

```text
crisis -> daily-life contrast -> investigation -> conflict -> emotional approach -> failure -> temporary victory -> consequence -> larger pressure
```

Macro pacing map:

* Act 1: hook, wound, world entry, first pressure, first choice, first cost
* Act 2A: investigation, training, social friction, desire, small wins, deeper rules
* Midpoint: major reveal, reversal, betrayal, false victory, or point of no return
* Act 2B: consequences, relationship strain, failed plan, moral compromise, pressure narrowing
* Act 3: convergence, sacrifice, final choice, climax, aftermath, new question

Adjust structure to the user's project. Do not force a rigid formula if the story needs another shape.

## Emotional Pacing

Slow down for moments that change identity, trust, love, grief, shame, desire, or self-image.

Do not skip over:

* betrayal
* death
* confession
* trauma
* intimacy
* major failure
* moral compromise
* first kill
* reunion
* irreversible choice

Emotional beat rhythm:

```text
event -> body reaction -> denial or defense -> behavior leak -> choice or silence -> consequence -> residue
```

Common fixes:

* Add silence, physical detail, avoidance, misdirected anger, repeated action, or an image.
* Let characters fail to say the true thing immediately.
* Let aftermath alter trust, plans, or self-image.
* Do not solve grief, shame, trauma, or intimacy in the same scene where it erupts.

## Information Pacing

Information should arrive when the reader wants it or when it creates a new problem.

Avoid:

* explaining magic, history, politics, factions, technology, or backstory before the reader has a reason to care
* answering the main mystery too early
* hiding every clue until the reveal
* using dialogue as a lecture
* making the protagonist understand everything without cost

Reveal through:

* action: a rule hurts someone
* mistake: the protagonist uses the wrong assumption
* clue: a detail is visible but ambiguous
* contradiction: two facts cannot both be true
* cost: learning the truth damages safety, trust, status, or hope
* misdirection: a true clue points to a plausible wrong answer

Information rhythm:

```text
question -> partial clue -> wrong interpretation -> consequence -> sharper question -> costly reveal
```

## Genre Pacing

Match rhythm to genre promise:

* Horror: delay certainty; escalate sensory wrongness; reveal traces before source; let consequences become irreversible.
* Mystery: give fair clues; alternate discovery and misdirection; reveal motive late but seed evidence early.
* Thriller: shorten decision windows; make safe places unsafe; let every answer create a bigger problem.
* Romance: use push-pull, vulnerability, restraint, misread signals, choice, and consequence; do not jump to confession without pressure.
* Zombie / post-apocalyptic: tighten resources over time; alternate external threat with group fracture and survival logistics.
* War: vary battle pressure with aftermath, command conflict, fatigue, supply, moral injury, and quiet cost.
* School story: use social rhythm: rumor, public embarrassment, exams, clubs, family pressure, status shifts.
* Mature 18+ psychological tension: build through restraint, boundaries, consent, power shifts, shame, desire, and aftermath; do not rush directly to heat.
* Game narrative: alternate objective, obstacle, choice, feedback, consequence, reward, and next hook.
