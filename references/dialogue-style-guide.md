# Dialogue Style Guide

Dialogue should reveal pressure, character, relationship, and subtext. It should not become a container for exposition the scene cannot carry.

## How to Avoid Exposition Dialogue

避免角色把設定塞進嘴裡的核心原則：角色只說他在當下有理由說的話。

Use these tactics:

* Move background information into conflict. Let a character accuse, hide, bargain, mock, or threaten with the information.
* Let one character know less than the other, but give them a personal reason to ask.
* Replace explanation with evidence: a scar, unpaid bill, forbidden room, broken device, old nickname, missing photo, ritual mark.
* Use partial information. People rarely explain the whole world; they refer to shared history in fragments.
* Make exposition cost something. If a character reveals a secret, they should risk trust, safety, status, or control.
* Cut the first explanatory line and start where the emotion begins.

Weak:

"As you know, our city has been sealed for ten years because the upper council banned all travel after the plague."

Stronger:

"你再碰那道門，我就當沒生過你。十年前你爸也是這樣出去的。"

## How to Make Characters Sound Different

Let voice come from social position, emotional habits, and what the character refuses to admit.

* age: Younger characters may use faster shifts, slang, deflection, exaggeration, or bluntness; older characters may use compression, authority, ritual politeness, or practiced silence.
* class: Wealth, precarity, labor, and access shape what characters notice and what they consider normal.
* education: Education affects abstraction, vocabulary, confidence, and how a character argues, but it should not erase personality.
* personality: Anxious characters over-explain or preempt blame; proud characters under-explain; playful characters deflect; controlling characters define terms.
* power position: Powerful characters can be brief, vague, or indirect; powerless characters may soften, flatter, joke, or choose exact words for safety.
* relationship: Intimates interrupt and use shorthand; strangers clarify; enemies weaponize history; family members know which small line hurts.
* emotional state: Fear shortens speech; anger sharpens it; shame dodges it; grief thins it; desire disguises it.
* region or culture: Use rhythm, idiom, politeness norms, taboo subjects, and code-switching carefully; avoid caricature.
* profession: Professionals notice field-specific details and use habits of command, diagnosis, negotiation, teaching, or performance.

## How People Speak Under Fear

Fear makes speech practical, fragmented, repetitive, or falsely calm.

Features:

* shorter sentences
* repeated questions
* unfinished thoughts
* denial of visible facts
* bargaining
* sudden obedience or sudden aggression
* focus on immediate sensory details

Example:

"別開。你聽，門外那個不是敲門。它在數。"

## How People Speak Under Anger

Anger narrows attention and turns speech into attack, accusation, command, or contempt.

Features:

* clipped lines
* direct blame
* insults or cold formality
* repeating the other person's words as accusation
* bringing up old debts
* refusing nuance

Example:

"你不是忘了。你是賭我會忍。這次你賭錯了。"

## How People Speak Under Shame

Shame makes people dodge, minimize, joke, lie, over-explain, or attack first.

Features:

* indirect answers
* self-mockery
* changing the topic
* too much explanation
* anger at being seen clearly
* small, defensive corrections

Example:

"我沒求他。我只是問價錢。問一下又不犯法。"

## How People Speak Under Grief

Grief often avoids the center of pain. It may focus on practical details because the truth is too large.

Features:

* ordinary details replacing direct emotion
* numbness
* repetition
* sudden irritation
* unfinished memory
* talking to the absent person as if present

Example:

"她的杯子還在水槽裡。你別洗。先別洗。"

## How People Speak Under Attraction

Attraction often hides inside attention, teasing, restraint, over-politeness, or a sudden inability to speak plainly.

Features:

* noticing small details
* indirect concern
* teasing that tests safety
* unfinished confession
* deflection after vulnerability
* asking practical questions with emotional weight

Example:

"你手在抖。別說沒有，我看到了。過來，我又不吃你。"

## How People Speak Under Exhaustion

Exhaustion strips language down. Characters may become blunt, numb, absurdly funny, or unable to perform their usual mask.

Features:

* shorter lines
* reduced politeness
* delayed reactions
* dark humor
* confession by accident
* inability to argue

Example:

"你要罵就快點。我站著都快睡著了。"

## Bad vs Improved Dialogue Examples

### 1. Fear

Bad:

"I am afraid because there is a monster outside and we may die."

Improved:

"別講話。它剛剛停在你名字那一格。"

### 2. Argument

Bad:

"You always ignore my feelings and make decisions without considering me."

Improved:

"你每次都說是為了大家。奇怪，受傷的怎麼都剛好不是你？"

### 3. Before Battle

Bad:

"We must fight bravely, because our destiny depends on this battle."

Improved:

"等他們靠近第三棵樹再放箭。誰先手抖，我親手把他踢下牆。"

### 4. After Battle

Bad:

"The battle was terrible, and I feel sad that our friends died."

Improved:

"他剛剛還在笑我綁繃帶太醜。你看，現在用不上了。"

### 5. Confession

Bad:

"I love you and have loved you for a long time."

Improved:

"我本來想等安全一點再說。後來發現我們這種人，哪有那一天。"

### 6. Concealment

Bad:

"I cannot tell you the truth because it would put you in danger."

Improved:

"你問第二次，我就得開始騙你。別逼我。"

### 7. Betrayal

Bad:

"I betrayed you because I had no choice."

Improved:

"我有選。只是每一個選項裡，死的人都不是我弟。"

### 8. Leadership Command

Bad:

"Everyone needs to stay calm and follow my orders."

Improved:

"看我。不是看門。看我。阿傑，帶三個人堵樓梯，現在。"

### 9. Group Panic

Bad:

"Everyone is scared and we should not panic."

Improved:

"誰把門閂拔了？我問誰拔的！別擠，後面沒有路！"

### 10. Dark Humor

Bad:

"This situation is bad, but I will make a joke."

Improved:

"好消息，電梯還能用。壞消息，它剛剛自己上來了。"
