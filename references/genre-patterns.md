# Genre Patterns

Use this reference when a story needs genre-aware drafting, diagnosis, outlining, or revision. For any genre, protect the genre promise while removing lazy clichés and strengthening emotional consequence.

## Horror

1. genre promise
   * Give the reader dread, vulnerability, uncertainty, bodily or psychological threat, and the feeling that safety is temporary.
2. reader expectations
   * Slow-burn unease, rules that are only partly understood, sensory distortion, isolation, escalation, and consequences that cannot be fully repaired.
3. common clichés
   * Characters ignoring obvious danger for no reason.
   * Monster reveals arriving too early.
   * Dreams, hallucinations, or jump scares replacing consequence.
   * Evil explained so much that it stops being frightening.
4. useful tension tools
   * Delay the reveal; let traces arrive before the source.
   * Use silence, wrong smells, repeated sounds, body sensations, and impossible details.
   * Make every explanation plausible but incomplete.
   * Let the threat change what characters are willing to do to survive.
5. revision questions
   * What is the reader afraid might happen before it happens?
   * What irreversible sign proves the world is no longer safe?
   * Where can the scene withhold certainty longer?
   * What does the horror cost the character besides physical survival?

## Zombie Apocalypse

1. genre promise
   * Deliver survival pressure, scarcity, bodily horror, social collapse, moral compromise, and group tension.
2. reader expectations
   * Logistics matter: food, water, infection, sleep, noise, weapons, transport, medicine, shelter, trust.
   * Human conflict should become as dangerous as the infected.
3. common clichés
   * Endless headshots without exhaustion or ammo limits.
   * The group arguing only to create noise.
   * Instant experts with no learning curve.
   * Infection rules changing whenever convenient.
4. useful tension tools
   * Force choices between speed and safety, mercy and quarantine, one person and the group.
   * Track resources visibly.
   * Make sound, smell, crowd movement, wounds, and fatigue shape action.
   * Use leadership disputes and rumor as accelerants.
5. revision questions
   * What resource is running out right now?
   * Who benefits from hiding the truth?
   * What is the infection rule, and who is testing it?
   * What moral line did survival push someone across?

## Dark Fantasy

1. genre promise
   * Offer wonder contaminated by cost, power tied to corruption or sacrifice, mythic pressure, and morally compromised choices.
2. reader expectations
   * Magic has limits and prices.
   * Institutions, gods, bloodlines, curses, bargains, and histories shape the present.
   * Victory rarely feels clean.
3. common clichés
   * Chosen-one certainty without cost.
   * Vague darkness replacing concrete culture.
   * Magic solving problems for free.
   * Cruelty used as decoration rather than consequence.
4. useful tension tools
   * Tie every power to debt, memory, body, reputation, blood, faith, or social consequence.
   * Make myths politically useful and partially false.
   * Let monsters reflect cultural sins, not just attack scenes.
   * Use sacred places, taboos, oaths, relics, and rituals as pressure points.
5. revision questions
   * What does power cost in this world?
   * Who profits from the myth the characters believe?
   * What moral compromise makes victory possible?
   * Where can the fantasy texture become more specific?

## Science Fiction

1. genre promise
   * Explore a premise through logic, social consequence, technological texture, and human cost.
2. reader expectations
   * The speculative idea should affect work, intimacy, class, politics, memory, body, identity, or survival.
   * The world should feel designed beyond the immediate plot.
3. common clichés
   * Techno-jargon replacing drama.
   * Inventions with no unintended consequences.
   * Societies that exist only to prove one theme.
   * Characters explaining the premise in lectures.
4. useful tension tools
   * Ask who controls the technology and who pays for it.
   * Turn technical limitations into plot pressure.
   * Let systems fail in plausible, specific ways.
   * Make private emotions collide with public-scale consequences.
5. revision questions
   * What social behavior changed because this premise exists?
   * What is the technology unable to do?
   * Who is excluded from its benefits?
   * What human need survives the futuristic surface?

## Romance

1. genre promise
   * Deliver desire, vulnerability, chemistry, emotional risk, obstacle, and meaningful consequence.
2. reader expectations
   * The central relationship must change both people.
   * Obstacles should expose wounds, values, class, duty, fear, or timing, not only misunderstanding.
   * Intimacy should be grounded in consent and character truth.
3. common clichés
   * Attraction described only through appearance.
   * Conflict based on an easily solved conversation.
   * One character losing agency to become a reward.
   * Chemistry stated rather than dramatized.
4. useful tension tools
   * Let attraction appear through attention, restraint, misread signals, small acts, and inconvenient honesty.
   * Make the obstacle emotionally rational for both sides.
   * Use power imbalance, social risk, old wounds, or incompatible futures carefully.
   * Let each intimate step create a consequence.
5. revision questions
   * Why are these two drawn to each other specifically?
   * What does each person risk by wanting the other?
   * What truth is one of them avoiding?
   * Does the scene create vulnerability, or only heat?

## Thriller

1. genre promise
   * Provide urgency, danger, reversals, pursuit, secrets, and decisions under time pressure.
2. reader expectations
   * Every scene should tighten the net.
   * Information should arrive with risk.
   * The protagonist should act, not only react.
3. common clichés
   * Villains explaining everything.
   * Convenient clues landing without cost.
   * Authorities being useless solely to isolate the hero.
   * Fake urgency without real deadlines.
4. useful tension tools
   * Use countdowns, surveillance, betrayal, narrowing options, and public/private stakes.
   * Let each answer create a bigger problem.
   * Make safe places compromised.
   * Force the hero to choose between bad options.
5. revision questions
   * What happens if the character waits ten minutes?
   * What does the protagonist do that changes the board?
   * What information is dangerous to know?
   * Where can a reversal make the scene less predictable?

## Mystery

1. genre promise
   * Give the reader a fair puzzle, meaningful clues, misdirection, motive, reveal timing, and trust that the solution was earned.
2. reader expectations
   * Clues should be visible but not obvious.
   * Suspects need motives, secrets, opportunities, and pressure.
   * The reveal should reframe earlier details.
3. common clichés
   * The culprit appears only at the end.
   * Detectives solve by intuition without evidence.
   * Red herrings have no character logic.
   * Coincidences replace deduction.
4. useful tension tools
   * Pair each clue with a misleading but plausible interpretation.
   * Give every suspect a secret, not every secret the crime.
   * Make evidence emotionally costly to obtain.
   * Use alibis, timelines, objects, habits, and omissions.
5. revision questions
   * Could the reader solve this with available evidence?
   * Which clue is fair but currently invisible?
   * What false story do the clues appear to tell?
   * Why does the culprit's action make emotional sense?

## War Story

1. genre promise
   * Explore violence, duty, fear, loyalty, loss, survival, command, moral injury, and the cost of political decisions on bodies.
2. reader expectations
   * Tactics, terrain, supply, fatigue, hierarchy, and fear should matter.
   * War changes relationships and self-image.
3. common clichés
   * Clean heroism without trauma or ambiguity.
   * Battle scenes with no spatial logic.
   * Soldiers speaking only in patriotic speeches.
   * Enemies reduced to faceless targets unless that dehumanization is intentionally examined.
4. useful tension tools
   * Track ammunition, communication, weather, wounds, orders, confusion, and misinformation.
   * Place loyalty against survival.
   * Let command decisions hurt specific people.
   * Use quiet aftermath scenes to show cost.
5. revision questions
   * What does the battlefield look like from the character's limited view?
   * Which order is tactically useful but morally damaging?
   * What bodily detail makes the violence real?
   * What does the character lose even if they survive?

## School Story

1. genre promise
   * Dramatize identity, belonging, status, friendship, rivalry, shame, ambition, secrecy, and first major choices inside a compressed social world.
2. reader expectations
   * Social consequences should feel immediate.
   * Small public moments can feel life-changing.
   * Authority, family, exams, clubs, rumors, money, and attraction shape pressure.
3. common clichés
   * Flat bully/victim dynamics.
   * Classes used only as background.
   * Teen characters speaking like adults giving essays.
   * Romance or conflict without social consequence.
4. useful tension tools
   * Use rumor networks, group chats, seating charts, exams, club politics, family expectations, and public embarrassment.
   * Let status change quickly and visibly.
   * Make secrets difficult to keep because everyone shares space.
   * Use ordinary objects as emotional triggers.
5. revision questions
   * What social cost does this choice create tomorrow?
   * Who is watching, recording, gossiping, or pretending not to care?
   * What does the character want others to believe about them?
   * How does the school setting intensify the conflict?

## Game Narrative

1. genre promise
   * Give the player motivated choices, readable stakes, memorable NPCs, consequence, lore through action, and a world that responds.
2. reader expectations
   * Goals should be playable, not only interesting in prose.
   * Dialogue should support agency, roleplay, pacing, and feedback.
   * Choices should reveal values and produce visible outcomes.
3. common clichés
   * Quest givers dumping lore before the player cares.
   * Fake choices with identical outcomes.
   * Factions with slogans instead of material interests.
   * Collect tasks with no story pressure.
4. useful tension tools
   * Give each quest a want, obstacle, twist, consequence, and optional moral angle.
   * Deliver lore through objects, rumors, environmental contradiction, and NPC need.
   * Let factions want incompatible futures.
   * Make rewards express values, not only numbers.
5. revision questions
   * Why should the player care before the lore dump?
   * What can the player choose that changes tone, access, allies, or endings?
   * Which NPC line gives motive in fewer words?
   * How does the world remember the player's action?

## Historical Fiction

1. genre promise
   * Make the past feel materially specific while dramatizing human pressure, social constraint, and irreversible choice.
2. reader expectations
   * Food, clothing, law, class, religion, labor, medicine, travel, and speech should shape conflict.
3. common clichés
   * Modern values pasted onto every character without cost.
   * Historical facts delivered as lectures.
   * Famous events used as wallpaper.
4. useful tension tools
   * Let social rule, technology, distance, illness, documents, and reputation create obstacles.
   * Make private desire collide with public consequence.
5. revision questions
   * What could this character not safely say in this era?
   * Which historical detail changes the choice, not only the scenery?

## Crime / Noir

1. genre promise
   * Deliver moral compromise, pressure from institutions or criminals, secrets, guilt, and a truth that costs something.
2. reader expectations
   * Motive, leverage, money, violence, class, corruption, and witness credibility matter.
3. common clichés
   * Cynical narration with no emotional wound.
   * Crime solved by coincidence.
   * Femme fatale or gangster stereotypes without agency.
4. useful tension tools
   * Give every alliance a price and every clue a risk.
   * Let the protagonist need something from people they should not trust.
5. revision questions
   * What truth is more dangerous than the crime?
   * What does the protagonist become willing to do?

## Wuxia / Xianxia

1. genre promise
   * Deliver martial or cultivation progression, honor, sect pressure, mythic scale, rivalry, debt, and moral tests.
2. reader expectations
   * Techniques, oaths, lineage, resources, realms, masters, rivals, and karmic cost should matter.
3. common clichés
   * Power-ups without cost.
   * Arrogant young masters repeated without variation.
   * Realm names replacing emotional stakes.
4. useful tension tools
   * Tie power to debt, body damage, reputation, sect law, forbidden technique, or lost memory.
   * Make honor and survival incompatible.
5. revision questions
   * What does advancement cost besides training time?
   * Which relationship becomes harder when the protagonist grows stronger?

## LitRPG / Progression

1. genre promise
   * Deliver measurable growth, strategic choice, build identity, challenge escalation, and satisfying feedback.
2. reader expectations
   * Systems must be consistent and choices must matter.
3. common clichés
   * Numbers replacing drama.
   * Grinding without character or plot change.
   * Overpowered abilities that erase tension.
4. useful tension tools
   * Make every upgrade exclude another path.
   * Let system rules create social, moral, or bodily cost.
5. revision questions
   * What build choice reveals character?
   * What can the system not solve?

## Court Intrigue

1. genre promise
   * Deliver status pressure, secrets, public performance, factional leverage, rumor, etiquette, and betrayal.
2. reader expectations
   * A glance, seat, gift, marriage, document, or title can change power.
3. common clichés
   * Everyone speaks in obvious threats.
   * Politics detached from food, money, marriage, law, military, or religion.
4. useful tension tools
   * Make every polite exchange carry a hidden transaction.
   * Track who knows what and who can prove it.
5. revision questions
   * What is said publicly and what is meant privately?
   * Who loses face if the truth appears?

## Comedy / Satire

1. genre promise
   * Deliver recognition, reversal, absurdity, contradiction, timing, and social bite.
2. reader expectations
   * Humor should expose character, system, hypocrisy, fear, or desire.
3. common clichés
   * Random jokes unrelated to pressure.
   * Characters becoming stupid only for a punchline.
4. useful tension tools
   * Let people pursue serious wants through absurd methods.
   * Use escalation, callback, status reversal, and bureaucratic logic.
5. revision questions
   * What truth does the joke reveal?
   * Does the humor increase or cancel pressure?

## Literary Fiction

1. genre promise
   * Deliver psychological truth, language, moral ambiguity, social observation, and emotional residue.
2. reader expectations
   * Inner change, perception, memory, relationship, and consequence matter as much as event.
3. common clichés
   * Beautiful vagueness without pressure.
   * Inaction mistaken for depth.
4. useful tension tools
   * Make small choices expose self-deception.
   * Use motif, memory, silence, and social detail as pressure.
5. revision questions
   * What truth is the character avoiding?
   * What changes by the end if the plot is quiet?

## Family Drama

1. genre promise
   * Deliver love, obligation, resentment, inheritance, memory, care, shame, loyalty, and old wounds.
2. reader expectations
   * History should enter through behavior, not exposition alone.
3. common clichés
   * One conversation fixes decades of pain.
   * Family members defined by one trait.
4. useful tension tools
   * Use repeated rituals, money, illness, caregiving, rooms, food, photos, and inherited objects.
5. revision questions
   * What can this family not say directly?
   * Which old debt shapes the current scene?

## Workplace Drama

1. genre promise
   * Deliver ambition, competence, hierarchy, status, burnout, ethics, teamwork, and institutional pressure.
2. reader expectations
   * Work should have real procedures, stakes, jargon in moderation, and consequences.
3. common clichés
   * Office setting as generic backdrop.
   * Villain boss with no system logic.
4. useful tension tools
   * Use deadlines, meetings, performance reviews, clients, budgets, errors, and public credit.
5. revision questions
   * What happens if the work fails?
   * Who benefits from the current process?

## Political / Legal / Medical

1. genre promise
   * Deliver institutional stakes, procedure, ethics, public consequence, private cost, and expert pressure.
2. reader expectations
   * Process matters: law, evidence, diagnosis, policy, chain of command, liability, consent, and records.
3. common clichés
   * Experts breaking rules without consequence.
   * Procedure ignored whenever inconvenient.
4. useful tension tools
   * Make the rule itself a source of conflict.
   * Put private compassion against professional duty.
5. revision questions
   * What rule blocks the emotionally obvious solution?
   * What record, witness, diagnosis, or vote changes the outcome?
